Detergent chemistry notes from the powder drawer

A practical chemistry card, rewritten from box backs and attendant notes. The point is not miracle whiteness. The point is getting soil to let go of cloth in municipal water.

Surfactant

tail: oily soil
head: water
job: pry grease into small suspended pieces so the tub can carry it away.

Too little leaves a gray film. Too much leaves foam that fools people into thinking cleaning is happening.

Builder

usual note: sodium carbonate or phosphate
job: soften hard water, raise alkalinity, help cotton release ordinary skin oil.

The 1981 shelf card says “phosphate works, river pays.” Someone underlined river three times.

Bleach column

Chlorine removes color by oxidation and punishes mistakes quickly. Oxygen bleach works slower, especially in cold water, and does not make ammonia into a gas problem.

Never mix chlorine bleach with ammonia. The penciled card did not use exclamation points; it used a box.

Optical brightener

Fluorescent dye absorbs ultraviolet light and reflects blue-white. It does not remove soil; it stages a cleaner-looking cloth under certain bulbs.
